BACKGROUND

We have developed a method that helps groups inside an organisation, or across disciplines, reach a deeper and more embodied understanding of each other’s hopes and fears for the future they share.

The intervention is experiential: based on interviews with each group, we craft two short immersive experiences per group — one of hope, one of fear. Participants visit their own experiences, then each other’s, with structured reflection between each crossing. What emerges is a shared encounter with the affective ground of the organisation’s decisions — and a foundation on which the more conventional work of strategy, change and innovation can land.

The intervention sits alongside, not instead of, the rational tools an organisation already uses. It changes what those tools have to work with.

EMPIRICAL

Julia Romanowska’s randomised comparative study at Karolinska Institutet (2014) is the first research to demonstrate that an art-based intervention produces measurable, lasting changes in leader behaviour — and that those changes ripple through to measurable improvements in employees’ mental health and biological stress markers (cortisol, DHEA-S). The work tested an intervention called Shibboleth against a conventional leadership programme. The differences were striking and sustained over long-term follow-up. This is, to our knowledge, the strongest published evidence base for the underlying mechanism.

THEORETICAL

C. Otto Scharmer’s Theory U frames major change as a movement from the surface — habitual responses — down into “presencing”: the moment of letting go and letting come, where new futures become possible. Rob Hopkins frames collective imagination as a civic muscle that needs deliberate exercise. Romanowska’s contribution — the notion of aesthemetics, the dialectical interweaving of aesthetic, emotional and ethical experience — provides the underlying theoretical engine.

PRACTICAL

Immersive experiential work has begun to find serious uptake at corporate and civic scale. SALLY by EY Doberman’s Dear Economy project, part-drama and part-escape-room, demonstrates that this kind of approach can be commissioned by serious organisations and produce serious outcomes. Our work anchors it in dedicated, research-backed methodology and applies it inside specific organisations to specific questions.
